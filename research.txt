I find a reusable “foundation concepts” core for strong, self-directed learners to be highly feasible—and, in some respects, easier to execute than a weak-learner core—provided the platform is designed around autonomy, challenge, and evidence of transfer, rather than remediation and heavy gating. The core architectural move is the same: represent teachable units (5–30 minutes) as nodes in a prerequisite-aware concept graph; the key difference is that for strong learners I would make prerequisites “soft-gated” (learner-confirmed with quick diagnostics) and organise learning around challenge ladders and portfolio evidence, with optional scaffolds that can be invoked on demand. This is consistent with evidence that while guidance is critical for novices, as expertise increases, the same guidance can become redundant or even counterproductive (the expertise reversal effect). 

I will adapt the instructional design away from “explain → practise” as default and towards “attempt → reflect → target practice → reattempt” (with guardrails), drawing on (a) research on self-regulated learning (planning, monitoring, self-reflection cycles), (b) evidence-based learning techniques for independent study (e.g., distributed practice, retrieval practice, elaboration), and (c) designs such as productive failure and desirable difficulties that can raise transfer when the learner has adequate prior knowledge. 

For the five subjects—Programming, English, German, Electricity, Robotics—I therefore recommend a two-layer concept system per subject:

Foundations: compact, high-transfer conceptual tools and representations that unlock most of the subject and support flexible problem solving (not just exam-style routines). These are still “foundations”, but tuned to depth: e.g., invariants and complexity reasoning (Programming), discourse structure and argument auditing (English), clause structure + modality and register (German), Kirchhoff reasoning and modelling (Electricity), state estimation and feedback thinking (Robotics). 
Extensions: short, modular “frontier glimpses” and skill-building mini-studios that invite self-directed exploration (e.g., concurrency and formal reasoning; corpus-style language inquiry; AC circuits and complex impedance; SLAM intuition; ethics and safety reasoning). I anchor these to official syllabi where possible (e.g., A-level specifications) and to widely recognised curricular frameworks (e.g., CS curriculum guidelines) to keep scope coherent.

Designing image and video support for teaching foundation concepts, including AI generation workflows
Executive summary
I find strong evidence that well-designed visuals (images, diagrams, animations, simulations) and short videos can materially improve learning of foundation concepts—especially when they are tightly aligned to objectives, minimise extraneous cognitive load, and are paired with active processing (retrieval practice, self-explanations, short tasks). This conclusion is strongly supported by the core learning-science frameworks of dual coding and multimedia learning, and by cognitive load theory. 

However, the same evidence base also shows that visuals can harm learning if they add “cost” without “benefit” (e.g., decorative pictures, split-attention layouts, redundancy, overly transient animations, or fast-paced video that encourages passive viewing). Learning benefits depend on design details, learner expertise, and the degree to which the medium forces or supports active processing. 

For learner differentiation, a key design implication is that “more support” is not always better: the expertise reversal effect predicts that features that help novices (integrated text, step-by-step worked guidance, heavy signalling) can become redundant and even impede more expert learners. This is directly relevant to a study platform that serves both weaker and stronger learners, because an image/video asset needs a variant strategy (or optional layers) rather than a one-size-fits-all design. 

On AI’s role (as of 2026-04-05), I assess AI as increasingly feasible for accelerating drafting and variation of visuals (storyboards, iconography, diagram clean-up, caption/transcript generation, voiceover, short B-roll clips), but not yet reliable enough to remove human validation for foundation content—because renderings can be plausible-but-wrong, biased, or legally/ethically risky (especially for likeness/voice and derivative-content). Governance and provenance (e.g., C2PA Content Credentials) are becoming standard expectations, and regulation is moving toward mandatory disclosure/marking of AI-generated content in the EU from 2 August 2026 for transparency obligations. 

A pragmatic path I recommend is a “human-in-the-loop” visual pipeline: template-driven manual production for the highest-stakes explanatory diagrams and worked examples; semi-automated production for screen-capture micro-videos and captioning; and AI-native generation for low-risk illustrative assets, alternative versions, and short generic B-roll—always with SME review gates and accessibility-by-design.
